Methodology

This process involved parsing the system requirements and case study documents to extract all 
nouns. These nouns were then filtered and categorized to distinguish between potential classes,

accurately reflects the business requirements without unnecessary clutter.

UML Class Diagram – Explanation 
To justify the structural decisions made in the diagram above, the following design principles 
were applied: 
 
 Key Classes: The model centers on Patient and Doctor as the primary actors. The 
Appointment class serves as the temporal link between them. We introduced Consultation to 
separate the scheduling aspect (Appointment) from the clinical aspect (Diagnosis/Notes). 
 Generalization (Inheritance): We utilized an abstract Person class to hold common 
attributes like name, address, and phone. Both Patient and Staff inherit from Person. 
Furthermore, Staff is a parent class for Doctor and Nurse. This reduces redundancy and 
allows for shared operations like login (). 
 Composition: A strong "part-whole" relationship exists between Patient and MedicalRecord. 
If a Patient is deleted from the system, their Medical Record implies no semantic 
meaning on its own; thus, we used Composition (*--). 
 Aggregation: We applied aggregation (o--) between Prescription and Medicine. While a 
prescription lists medicines, the Medicine (inventory item) exists independently of any 
single prescription. 
 
 
Modeling Assumptions 
The following assumptions were made to define the boundaries and cardinalities of the domain 
model: 
 
 Single Medical Record: Each patient has exactly one MedicalRecord that aggregates all 
their history; they do not have separate records for different visits. 
 Appointment Exclusivity: An Appointment is strictly between one Doctor and one Patient at a 
time (no group therapy sessions). 
 Billing Trigger: A Bill is generated specifically from a Consultation, not just from an 
appointment request. 
 Prescription Items: A single Prescription can include multiple Medicine items, but must 
include at least one (Multiplicity 1. *).

Brief Explanation 
The state machine above models the lifecycle of a Bill object. A bill is first created from a completed 
consultation and issued to the patient entering the Pending Payment state. From there it may 
become Partially Paid or fully Paid, or transition to Overdue when the due date passes without full 
payment. Administrative actions can cancel a bill, while long-overdue bills are eventually Written 
Off. This lifecycle is directly driven by the Make payment, Generate monthly bill, 
and maintenance use cases (e.g., cleanup of expired or uncollectible bills).

• State Machine #2 – Appointment Lifecycle: 
The Appointment lifecycle is modeled with a composite Active state (Verified, AlternativeProposed, 
Confirmed). Within Confirmed, concurrent regions capture Billing (Pending → Paid/Failed with retry) 
and Notifications (Scheduled → Sent). Guards such as [>24h] encode cancellation policies, no-show

slot, Complete patient treatments, Cancel appointment, Send appointment reminders, and Clean up 
expired slots.

State Machine #2 – Appointment Lifecycle (Explanation) 
This state machine models the full lifecycle of an Appointment in the Healthcare System. An 
appointment is first created in the Requested state when a booking request is made. Inside the composite 
Active state, the system verifies the request; if the chosen slot is unavailable, the flow passes through 
AlternativeProposed until a new slot is selected. Once the appointment is accepted, it moves to 
Confirmed, which contains two concurrent regions: Billing (tracking payment from Pending through 
Paid or Failed with retry) and Notify (scheduling and sending reminders). 
From Confirmed, the appointment can progress to InProgress and then Completed when the consultation 
is finished, or it can transition to Cancelled (e.g., cancelByUser()[>24h]) or Expired if no payment or 
confirmation occurs before a timeout. This lifecycle ties directly to the Book appointment, Confirm 
appointment slot, Cancel appointment, Complete patient treatments, Send appointment reminders, and 
billing-related use cases, ensuring that all temporal and payment-related behaviors of appointments are 
explicitly captured.

Activity Diagrams – Explanations for Report 
 
The following activity diagrams were developed to illustrate control flow, decision logic, and 
system–actor interactions for each complex use case. The diagrams highlight branching 
conditions, exception handling, and synchronization points where system behaviors (e.g., 
validations, notifications, gateway calls) occur. 
 
UC-01 Book Appointment – Activity Diagram Explanation 
Primary Flow Activity Diagram 
The activity diagram for Book appointment models the operational steps undertaken by the 
Patient and the Healthcare System to secure an appointment slot. 
The flow begins when the Patient accesses the booking function. The system presents available 
specialties and date filters, after which the Patient selects a doctor and a preferred time range. 
The system retrieves matching slots and displays them. 
The Patient selects a slot, triggering system validation of both the user’s information and the slot 
availability. A decision node evaluates whether the clinic requires prepayment; if so, the Make 
payment activity is invoked. Upon successful validation (and payment if required), the system 
creates the Appointment, reserves the Room/slot, updates the appointment status, and proceeds to 
send a confirmation notification. The flow concludes with the Patient receiving confirmation 
details. 
Alternative Flow – Slot Unavailable 
The alternative activity path captures the case where the selected slot becomes unavailable 
during validation. After the system detects unavailability, a branch directs the flow to notify the 
patient and refresh slot availability by invoking Update slot availability. The system presents 
the updated list of free slots, and the Patient re-enters the selection step. The flow rejoins the 
main path once a valid slot is selected, leading to appointment creation, reservation, and 
confirmation.

UC-06 Cancel Appointment – Activity Diagram Explanation 
Primary Flow Activity Diagram 
The activity diagram for Cancel appointment highlights the decision-based cancellation process 
involving either a Patient or a Receptionist. The flow begins when the actor opens the

appointment). If the cancellation is permitted, the system updates the Appointment status to 
Cancelled, frees the associated Room/slot, triggers cancellation notifications to Patient and 
Doctor, and displays a confirmation to the user. 
Alternative Flow – Late Cancellation 
The alternative activity path addresses policy violations when the cancellation request occurs too 
close to the appointment time. After evaluating the cancellation window, the system displays a

Alternative Flow – Payment Failure 
The alternative branch corresponds to the Handle payment failure extension. If the Payment 
Gateway returns a failure response, the system displays an error message and logs the failure. A 
user decision node offers two paths: Retry or Cancel. 
If the Patient retries, the flow returns to the payment request step (potentially after choosing a 
different payment method). Upon eventual success, the flow rejoins the primary scenario. 
If the Patient cancels, the system retains the Bill in PendingPayment, increments any failure 
counters as required, and ends the flow with the bill remaining unpaid.

System Sequence Diagrams – Design Approach  
 
For the remaining use cases (UC‑02 to UC‑12 and UC‑T1–T3), System Sequence Diagrams 
(SSDs) were created to capture the external interactions between actors and the Healthcare 
System at the system boundary. Each SSD uses a single HealthcareSystem lifeline to represent 
the system as a black box, together with the relevant external actors such as Patient, Receptionist, 
Doctor, System scheduler, and Administrator. Additional external services (for example, 
NotificationService or ReportingService) are modeled as separate lifelines only when their 
behaviour is visible at the system level. 
 
The primary scenario for each use case was taken from the refined Deliverable 2 descriptions. 
Individual steps in the main flow were translated into high‑level messages rather than low‑level 
UI events. Actor‑initiated actions are modeled as requests to HealthcareSystem (for example, 
registerPatient(data), manageSchedule(), confirmSlot(), authorizeDischarge()), while system 
responses are modeled as messages back to the actor (for example, showForm(), 
displaySchedule(), showConfirmation()). Where the system invokes background or external 
behaviour (such as sending reminders or generating reports), additional messages are sent from 
HealthcareSystem to the corresponding service lifeline. 
 
To ensure consistency across the whole model, the SSDs follow the same naming and style 
conventions as those prepared for the three complex use cases (UC‑01 Book appointment, UC‑06 
Cancel appointment, UC‑07 Make payment). The system lifeline is always named 
HealthcareSystem, message names use clear verb–object patterns (requestX(), showY(), 
updateZ()), and no low‑level GUI clicks are shown. Each use case has at least one primary SSD 
covering the everything‑goes‑right scenario; important alternative or exception paths (such as 
invalid input, conflicts in schedules, or failures in background jobs) are represented using UML 
alt fragments within the same diagram. Diagrams are exported with consistent naming (for 
example, SSD_UC02_RegisterPatient_Primary, SSD_UC03_ManageSchedule_Primary) and are 
referenced throughout the report using these identifiers.

patient data consistent with an external system. The Receptionist initiates synchronization for a 
given patient, the HealthcareSystem exchanges data with an ExternalPatientSystem, and then 
either updates the local Patient and MedicalRecord or reports an error. The alt fragment 
distinguishes successful synchronization from failures such as connectivity or validation 
problems.
